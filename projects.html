My commitment to research has resulted in my designation as a Science Research Fellow in the Columbia Undergraduate Scholars Program.

The Morphogenesis and Developmental Biomechanics Lab

I am a research assistant in the The Morphogenesis and Developmental Biomechanics Lab in the Department of Biomedical Engineering at Columbia University. I am working on two projects investigating the morphogenesis of the midbrain-hindbrain boundary (MHB) of the primitive brain tube in the chick embryo: 1) Using gain and loss of function experiments and live imaging to determine the role of Fgf8 as a modulator of endoderm tension 2) Building a cantilever to quantitatively measure surface stress by deflection of midbrain tissue.

Department of Neuroscience

I have been eager to pursue research opportunities to develop my research skills and acumen. For example, this past summer I was a Research and Early Development intern in the Neuroscience department at Genentech, Inc., in South San Francisco. My primary project sought to test various CRISPR/Cas9 techniques in order to optimize CRISPR/Cas9 gene knockout in primary microglia cultures for genome-wide screening for neurodegenerative diseases. I found research in industry to be incredibly collaborative and mission-oriented, and I highly valued the opportunity to acquire knowledge of patent law, clinical trials, and drug development beyond early discovery.

The Berchowitz Laboratory

During the Fall of 2017 I have been an undergraduate researcher at the Berchowitz Laboratory at the Columbia University Medical Center, researching meiosis-induced amyloid formation and clearance in the yeast model. I am responsible for identifying hits from a genome-wide genetic screen that may play a role in the folding of amyloid-like structures. Through my research, I’ve become skilled in using immunofluorescence microscopy and western blotting to visualize and analyze the effects of genetic hit-knockouts on the progression of meiosis.

The Galloway Laboratory

During the summer of 2017, I worked at the Harvard Stem Cell Institute in the Galloway Laboratory, studying tendon regeneration. I was responsible for developing a reporter cell line in embryonic stem cells for tendon lineage. I used UCSC genome browser and Serial Cloner to design and visualize vectors, designed and ordered guide RNAs for PCR, learned how to employ Gibson Assembly. I was responsible for maintaining cultures of mouse embryonic 10T1/2 cells, and measured gene expression of cellular proliferation factors of cells under mechanical stress via qPCR. I was accepted to give an oral presentation at the MIT IEEE Undergraduate Research Technology conference in November 2017, and was accepted to give an oral presentation at the National Conference on Undergraduate Research in April 2018.

The Lappalainen Laboratory

In the Summer of 2016, I interned at the New York Genome Center, assigned to a project editing SNPs modulating expression quantitative trait loci (eQTLs) using CRISPR/Cas9 and designing templates for homology directed repair. I maintained human cell cultures, CRISPR/Cas9 transfection, and next-generation sequencing library preparation to confirm SNP edits.

Institute for Regenerative Engineering

I became fascinated with biotechnology after working at the Institute for Regenerative Engineering at the University of Connecticut Health Center for two years, engineering scaffolds and microparticles for drug delivery of green tea antioxidants for cartilage regeneration. During my time there I completed two independent research projects that I had the opportunity to present at both regional and national science fairs such as the Intel International Science and Engineering Fair. I learned laboratory techniques such as cell culture and how to use interpret results from analytical tools such as DSC, SEM and FT-IR, and participated in laboratory journal club.
